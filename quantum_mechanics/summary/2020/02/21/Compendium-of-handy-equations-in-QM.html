Schrödinger equation

\[i\hbar \frac{d\Psi(x,t)}{dt} = \hat{\mathcal{H}} \Psi(x,t)\]

Constants and values

Flux quantum:

The wave function and probabilities

The Hamiltonian

If $\ket{n}$ is an eigenstate of the Hamiltonian:

\[\bra{n}\hat{H}\ket{n} = E_n\]

\(\bra{n}\ket{m} = 1\) if $m=n$

Operators

In the Heisenberg picture $A_H = U^\dagger(t) A_S U(t)$ (incl. for $\hat{H}$)

\[\frac{d}{dt}A_H(t) = \frac{i}{\hbar}[H_H(t),A_H(t)] + \left(\ptpt{A_S}{t}\right)_H\]

Observables are Hermitian

The energy of a particle is always $E>V_{min}$ in the Time-independent Schrodinger equation.

The eigenfunctions/values with a discrete spectra of a Hermitian operator are always real.

The eigenfunctions/values with a discrete spectra of a Hermitian operator are always orthonormal, if they are degenerate, we can make them orthonormal.

The eigenfunction/values of a discrete spectra of a Hermition operator with finite dimensional values is complete.

The eigenfunction of a continous spectrum with a real eigenvalue is Dirac orthonormal ($\bra{f_x}\ket{f_y} = \delta(x-y)$)

The normalizable eigenfunctions/eigenfunctions in Hilbert space of a continous spectrum has real eigenvalues.

Commutation

Generalized uncertainty relation (with operators $A$ and $B$) ($\Delta A = \langle A^2 \rangle - \langle A \rangle^2$):

\[\Delta A \Delta B \geq \left( \frac{\langle [A,B]\rangle}{2i}\right)^2\]

Ultra generalized uncertainty relation (with operators $A$ and $B$) ($\Delta A = \langle A^2 \rangle - \langle A \rangle^2$):

\[\Delta A \Delta B \geq \frac{\langle -i[A,B]\rangle^2+\langle \{A,B\} - 2\expval{A}\expval{B}\rangle^2}{4}\]

If \(\left[\hat{A},\hat{B}\right]=0\) and $\ket{\psi}$ is an eigenfunction of $\hat{A}$ with eigenvalue $A_n$ then $\hat{B}\ket{\psi}$ is also an eigenfunction of $\hat{A}$ with eigenvalue $A_n$ (and vice versa)

\[\hat{A}(\hat{B}\ket{n}_A) = \hat{B}(\hat{A}\ket{n}_A)= \hat{B}(A_n \ket{n}_A) = A_n(\hat{B}\ket{n}_A)\]

Perturbation theory

For $\hat{H} = \hat{H}_0 + \hat{H}’$ where the eigenstates/eigenenergies to $\hat{H}_0$ are $E^0_n$ and $\psi^0_n$ and a non-degenerate energy level:

\[E_n^1 = \bra{\psi_n^0}H'\ket{\psi_n^0}\]

Second order perturbation energy diff:

\[E_n^2 = \sum_{m\neq n} \frac{\lvert\bra{\psi^0_n}H'\ket{\psi^0_m}\rvert^2}{E_n^0 - E_m^0}\]

Pre-fermi’s Golden Rule ($H_0 + H’(t)$, $\ket{\psi(t)} = \sum_n a_n(t) e^{iE^0_nt/\hbar}\ket{\psi_n^0}$):

\[i\hbar\ptpt{a_k(t)}{t} = \sum_n a_n(t) \bra{\psi_k^0}H'\ket{\psi_n^0}e^{it(E^0_k-E^0_n)/\hbar}\]

Fermi’s Golden Rule (from initial eigenstate $\ket{i}$ to all other continuum eigenstates $\ket{n}$)
where the coupling $g(E_n)$ of $n$ and $i$ is continuous over $n$
where $t<t_H = \frac{2\pi \hbar}{\Delta}\approx 2\pi\hbar \rho(E_i)$, with $\Delta$ the energy spacing of the ‘continuum’
and where $E_i$ far from continuum thresholds $t\gg\frac{2\pi \hbar}{|E_b-a|}$
and where $H’\ll H_0$:

\[\Gamma_{i\rightarrow cont} = \frac{2\pi}{\hbar} \sum_n |g_{n}|^2 \delta (E_i-E_n) \approx \frac{2\pi}{\hbar} |g(E_i)|^2 \rho_{DOS}(E_i)\]

$g(E_n) = g_n = \bra{n}H’\ket{i}$, $\rho(\epsilon)$ the density of states, $t_H$ called the Heisenberg time.

Second order Fermi’s Golden rule (virtual excitations):

\[\Gamma_{i\rightarrow f}^{(2)} = \frac{2\pi}{\hbar} |\sum_v \frac{H'_{fv}H'_{vi}}{E_v-E_i}|^2 \delta (E_i-E_f)\]

Gauge invariance

Analytical solutions

Landau levels (energy):

\[E_n = \frac{1}{2}\omega_B(n+\frac{1}{2}) \qquad \omega_B = \lvert\frac{qB}{m}\rvert\]

Zeeman splitting (spin 1/2, free electrons, g=2):

\[E = g\mu_B B \qquad \mu_B = \frac{e\hbar}{2m}\]

Two-electron system spin operator eigenstates of \(\vec{S}^2 = \vec{S}^2_1 + \vec{S}_2^2 = S_x^2+S_y^2+S_z^2\) and \(S_z = S_{z1}+S_{z2}\)

\(S^2 = 0, S_z =0\qquad \ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{\uparrow\downarrow}-\ket{\downarrow\uparrow})\qquad (singlet)\) \(S^2 = 1, S_z =1\qquad \ket{\psi} = \ket{\uparrow\uparrow}\qquad (triplet)\) \(S^2 = 1, S_z =0\qquad \ket{\psi} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{\uparrow\downarrow}+\ket{\downarrow\uparrow})\qquad (triplet)\) \(S^2 = 1, S_z =-1\qquad \ket{\psi} = \ket{\downarrow\downarrow}\qquad (triplet)\)

Eigenvalues of \(\vec{S}_1\cdot \vec{S}_2\):

\[\langle \vec{S}_1\cdot \vec{S}_2 \rangle_{triplet} = \frac{1}{4}\hbar^2 \qquad \langle \vec{S}_1\cdot \vec{S}_2 \rangle_{singlet} = -\frac{3}{4}\hbar^2\]

Hamiltonian that gives the eigenstates and eigenvalues of the 2-spin operator $\mathbf{S}_1\cdot \mathbf{S}_2$ is:

\[\hat{H} = \frac{1}{4}(E_s+3E_t) - (E_s - E_t) \mathbf{S}_1\cdot \mathbf{S}_2 = C - J \mathbf{S}_1\cdot \mathbf{S}_2\]

with $E_s = -3/4\hbar^2 $ and $E_t = 1/4 \hbar^2$ and $J = (E_s - E_t)$

Calculation tools of quantum mechanics

Adiabatic theorem:
If we place a system in a non-degenerate eigenstate $\ket{n}$ and vary the parameters of the Hamiltonian sufficiently slowly, the system will stay in the eigenstate $\ket{n}$ if no level-crossings are encountered.

Berry connection $\mathcal{A}_j(\lambda)$(under adiabatic change of parameters $\lambda^j$ of eigenstate $\ket{n}(\lambda(t))$ with constant energy $E_n(\lambda(t)) = 0$)ref:

\[\mathcal{A}_j(\lambda) = -i\bra{n}\frac{\partial \ket{n}}{\partial \lambda^j}\]

Berry phase: the phase acquired (next to the energy dynamical phase) by changing parameters $\lambda^j$ adiabatically:

\[e^{i\gamma} = \exp(-i\oint_C\mathcal{A}_j (\lambda)d\lambda^j)\]

‘Gauge’ invariance of Berry connection (with starting state $\ket{n’} = e^{i\omega(\lambda)}\ket{n}$:

\[\mathcal{A}'_j(\lambda) = \mathcal{A}_j(\lambda) + \frac{\partial \omega(\lambda)}{\partial \lambda^j}\]

Berry curvature (all physical phase information):

\[\mathcal{F}_{ij}= \ptpt{\mathcal{A}_j}{\lambda^j}\]

Berry curvature quantization theorem:
For any closed surface $S$, we find that $\oint_S \mathcal{F}_{ij} dS^{ij} =2 \pi k$ for $k\in\mathcal{Z}$

Postulates of quantum mechanics

There exist identical particles are indistinguishable -> Permutation of every pair of identical particles in a system follows the same Hamiltonian

For a system of $N$ identical particles, the symmetry posulate requires it to be fully symmetric or fully anti-symmetric (there could be some subspaces in which you could work otherwise, giving \hat{P}_{123}\psi=e^{i2\pi/3}\psi).

Other useful theorems of quantum mechanics

For identical particles: the Hamiltonian is invariant under permutation (operator $\hat{P}_{ij}$, where $\lambda_P = \pm 1$ and $\hat{P}^2 = 1$ and $\hat{P} = \hat{P}^\dagger$): \(\hat{H}(r_1,r_2,...,r_i,...,r_j,...,r_N)= \hat{H}(r_1,r_2,...,r_j,...,r_i,...,r_N) \Leftrightarrow \hat{P}_{ij}\hat{H} \hat{P}_{ij} = \hat{H} \Leftrightarrow [\hat{P},\hat{H}]=0\)

Time reversal symmetry (defined as \(\hat{\mathcal{T}}\hat{\mathcal{H}}= \hat{\mathcal{H}}\hat{\mathcal{T}}\)) using the Schrodinger equation:

\[\hat{\mathcal{T}} = \hat{\mathcal{K}} (when no spin)\] \[\hat{\mathcal{T}} = i\sigma^y \hat{\mathcal{K}} (with spin)\]

where $\hat{\mathcal{K}}$ is the complex conjugation operator. Other time-reversal results:

\[\hat{\mathcal{T}}\vec{\mu} = -\vec{\mu}\] \[E_{EM}(x,t) \propto e^{-ikz}\qquad\hat{\mathcal{T}}E_{EM}(x,t) = E_{EM}(x,-t) \propto e^{+ikz}\]

Fermion permutation operator: \(\Psi(r_1,r_2,...,r_i,...,r_j,...,r_N)= -1 \Psi(r_1,r_2,...,r_j,...,r_i,...,r_N)\)

Boson permutation operator: \(\Psi(r_1,r_2,...,r_i,...,r_j,...,r_N)= +1 \Psi(r_1,r_2,...,r_j,...,r_i,...,r_N)\)

Anyon permutation operator ($\theta_{ij}=\pi (1-\frac{1}{v}), v\in\mathbb{Z}$): \(\Psi(r_1,r_2,...,r_i,...,r_j,...,r_N)= e^{i\theta_{ij}} \Psi(r_1,r_2,...,r_j,...,r_i,...,r_N)\)

For symmetric potentials, the wavefunctions are alternatively even and odd (ground state even, 1st state odd, etc.)\

Every more excited state has an extra zero-crossing in the wavefunction.\

Most stationary state solutions (wavefunctions) of the Hamiltonian are orthogonal.

Most stationary states are complete.
